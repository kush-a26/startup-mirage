THE STARTUP
MIRAGE

India registered over three lakh startups and called it a revolution. Follow the money, and most of the country vanishes.

↓ scroll to begin
16 January 2016 · Vigyan Bhawan, New Delhi

"We want to make India a nation of job creators, not job seekers."

Prime Minister Modi stood before India's entrepreneurial class and announced a movement. Not a scheme — a movement. One that would ignite a spark in every corner of Bharat.

"Startup India is not just a scheme. It is a movement. A movement that will ignite the spark of entrepreneurship in every corner of Bharat."
— PM Modi, Startup India Launch, 2016

The programme moved fast. Startups received tax exemptions, faster incorporation, access to government tenders. A Fund of Funds worth ₹10,000 crore was committed to seed venture capital. India climbed to third in global startup rankings.

By every official measure, it succeeded. The map glowed.

But registered is not the same as funded. The government drew one map. Venture capital drew another. They barely overlap.

Chapter 4 — The mechanism

It was never about geography. It was about who you knew.

Bengaluru didn't attract capital because something in the water produces good startups. It attracted capital because the people who write the cheques, and the people who receive them, went to the same seven campuses.

Of India's 108 unicorn companies, 60 have at least one IIT-alumni founder — 55% of the country's billion-dollar startups traced back to seven institutions that admit roughly 16,000 students a year from a country of 1.4 billion. The IIMs add another layer. Together, a single pipeline produced the majority of India's most valuable companies.

Startup India registered three lakh founders. It did not touch the network that decides which ones get capital. That network was already in place, sorting for the same credentials, long before 2016.

The problem is not that the programme failed to register startups. It registered them by the lakh. The problem is that registration and funding are two entirely different worlds — and the gap between them follows a precise geography.

By October 2025, over 6,300 recognised startups had officially closed. Approximately 90% of Indian startups fail within five years. The certificate is not a safety net. It is paperwork.

The other India

The founders the capital never reached

The YNOS database records their names. Hundreds of startups in Nagaland, Bihar, Rajasthan — registered, categorised, given a sector tag. They did everything the programme asked. They incorporated, filed compliance returns, waited for the system to notice.

The data does not tell us their ideas were bad. It tells us their ideas existed outside the network that decides what is worth funding. No IIT alumni in the founding team. No warm intro to a Bengaluru-based partner.

The programme registered three lakh founders and called it a movement. The capital reached seven hundred and eight of them. The rest received a certificate, a sector tag, and silence.

The system wasn't broken. It was working exactly as designed — for the people it was already designed for.
