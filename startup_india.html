The Startup
Mirage

India registered over three lakh startups and called it a revolution. Follow the money, and most of the country vanishes.

↓ scroll to begin
January 16, 2016 · Vigyan Bhawan, New Delhi

"We want to make India a nation of job creators, not job seekers."

Prime Minister Narendra Modi stood before India's entrepreneurial class and made a promise. The Startup India programme would be the engine of a new economy — one that reached every district, every state, every young person with an idea and a laptop.

"Startup India is not just a scheme. It is a movement. A movement that will ignite the spark of entrepreneurship in every corner of Bharat."

The programme moved fast. Within three years, over 27,000 crore rupees had been committed through the Fund of Funds for Startups. By 2024, the government had recognised over 3,12,866 startups — granting them tax exemptions, faster incorporation, and access to government tenders. India climbed to third in global startup rankings. The scheme, by every official metric, was a success.

But registered is not the same as funded. Recognised is not the same as backed. And the government's map of India's startup revolution looks very different from the map that venture capital drew.

— PM Modi, Startup India Launch, 2016

The numbers seemed to confirm it. By 2024, over three lakh startups had registered under the programme. Government press releases glowed. India had become the third-largest startup ecosystem in the world. But registered is not the same as funded.

Chapter 2 — Where the money went

Only 708 startups received any investor backing.

0.23% of all registered startups — 708 out of 3,12,866

The rest of the country — in amber — registered, applied, and waited. The darkest states received the most capital. Most of India sits in the shadows.

Chapter 3 — The three-state story

Karnataka, Maharashtra and Tamil Nadu hold 56% of all VC-funded startups.

7.28% Karnataka's VC conversion rate — the highest in India

A founder in Karnataka is 40 times more likely to receive venture capital than one in Bihar. The three outlined states dominate India's entire funding ecosystem.

The other India

The founders the capital never reached

The YNOS database records their names. Hundreds of startups in Nagaland, Bihar, Rajasthan — registered, categorised, given a sector tag. They did everything the programme asked. They built products, filed papers, waited for the system to notice.

The data does not tell us their ideas were bad. It tells us that the capital flowed overwhelmingly to a small set of cities, connected by alumni networks and proximity to investors concentrated in Bengaluru, Mumbai, and Delhi.
